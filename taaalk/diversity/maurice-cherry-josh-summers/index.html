Exploring The Concept Of Diversity

@mauricecherry

Maurice Cherry

Maurice is the founder and creative principal of 3eighteen media, a design and consulting company in Atlanta's West End neighbourhood. He is highly active across a range of diversity projects. He is the founder of the Black Weblog Awards, Revision Path (a weekly interview showcasing Black graphic and web designers), and 28 Days of the Web (a featured list of a different Black designers produced in conjunction with, and celebration of, Black History Month).

Maurice is also an educator, and has taught courses on web design and WordPress for Mediabistro, DeVry University, and other institutions. He also has a daily short-burst podcast called The Year of Tea where he reviews a different tea each day. He is a past contributor for Site5, SitePoint, 10,000 Words, and Black Web 2.0.

Josh Summers

Josh Summers is one of the founders of Taaalk and on the Investment Team at DN Capital. Having lived a lot of his life not having to fight any sort of negative bias, he has (ashamedly) not spent a great deal of time exploring the concept of diversity - which is why doing this Taaalk was so appealing. He blogs at joshsummers.co.uk, has a BSc in Economics from University College London, strong interests in Computer Science and Psychology and paid for his time at university by playing copious amounts of online poker.

Josh Summers

This is somewhat of a stupid question - because I personally believe that it is; but why do you think diversity is important?

Maurice Cherry

Before we get into that, I think it's important to properly frame the concept of diversity. Diversity encompasses a spectrum of thingsâ€”gender, race, ethnicity, age, sexual orientation, biological sex, nationality, language, spirituality, physical appearance, etc.

So when people ask "why is diversity important?", it's because we're all different! This "melting pot" that we call the United States, for example, is a rich mix of cultures that influence everything from the food we eat to the policies that shape our country. Big, tall, short, small, Latino, Black, young, old, etc. -- we all have experiences and insight and knowledge borne out of these differences.

Diversity is important because those differences are what make us great.

Josh Summers

I feel you. I'd never thought of it that way. I'd always connected diversity with the idea of a struggle instead of a celebration.

Where do you see it on that scale? Do you feel like you're fighting for something (there are all these different types of people, the world is better when everyone's voice is heard), against something (there's a pressure against diversity that needs to be battled) or somewhere in the middle?

Maurice Cherry

It's always a back and forth battle between those two states, and based on one's perspective, it can look different. People fight against tradition and people fight against progress. I don't know if there's ever going to be a true state of equilibrium.

What's different now is that the voices which were once in the minority now have the tools to make their voices heard just as much as those who have always held a place of power.

Josh Summers

That's true. The internet has done a wonderful job in terms of equalizing the distribution of voice.

For you how is it? A feeling of celebration or struggle? Or do you feel yourself also moving back and forth between those two states?

Maurice Cherry

Oh, I definitely go back and forth! Anyone who does any sort of advocacy work knows you have to take the good with the bad. It's part of the job. Some days it's great! People see where you're coming from and what you're trying to do and support you wholeheartedly. And then there are days when you feel like you're the only one speaking out and no one's listening.

Josh Summers

I can imagine!

I just want to quickly back up to the start of us talking again and ask the same question I started with. I understand now that diversity is not about one group vs another but it's about the creation of the cocktail. What do you think that cocktail brings to the table that means it's worth working towards? (Again, I do believe it *is* worth it - I just want to hear your perspective). Why is diversity important?

Maurice Cherry

One of the main benefits that diversity brings to the table is different viewpoints and different ways to solve problems. We live in a multicultural world, so it makes sense to have groups reflect the world we live in. And while diversity is important, inclusion is just as much of a factor.

For businesses, there are even studies which show that a diverse workforce (in terms of race and gender) is directly related to increased profits, revenue, more customers, higher market share, and greater profitability. What business wouldn't want those benefits?

Josh Summers

No, I don't think any sensible business wouldn't want that. More perspectives on a situation pretty much always leads to a better result. What do you mean when you say inclusion is just as much as a factor?

Also, when you're working on diversity related things, what is your mission? Say you were a startup, their might be some metric (or action) you'd be aiming for your users to achieve. Is this how you think about things? And if it is, what is that metric? Is it a realisation about the benefits of diversity in people's minds (perhaps those that haven't thought much about it), or is it more tangible, say every company in your area should have a diverse board of directors?

Maurice Cherry

Inclusion is important because it helps foster a culture that supports and accepts diverse viewpoints. Having "diversity for diversity's sake" -- so to speak -- is futile if there's not a consideration for inclusion.

I'll give you an example. Let's look at some of the big Silicon Valley tech companies who have had these dismal single digit diversity percentages as it relates to their U.S.-based workforces. Some of these companies are working to correct this by increasing recruiting efforts, supporting coding bootcamps, etc. So in the next few years, we will start to see these numbers change for the better. Inclusion is important in this case because their corporate cultures will end up shifting to accommodate these demographic changes. It would be a huge disservice to have put forth the effort to diversify your workforce only to have your workplace culture end up driving those same people away.

I want to be clear though -- I am not a diversity consultant. I can give advice based off of my own experiences and from the experiences of dozens of designers and developers I've talked with, but it's just my advice. Maybe it will work. Maybe it won't. Your mileage may vary.

But let me answer your question about what happens when I work on diversity-related projects. The projects I've done (Black Weblog Awards, Revision Path) have been about showcasing, uplifting, and motivating Black digital creative people. That's the primary mission. I'm not looking at in terms of metrics or any other numbers like that. For me, success means that others are able to shine based off of this platform that I've built to recognize and celebrate their hard work, creativity, and sacrifice.

Josh Summers

Between the ages of 16-20 I played a lot of poker, and one of the things that I loved about poker was that it was the game didn't care about who you were, only the work you put in and the results you generated. In the game everything gets stripped away apart from your actions, and those that do the best in the long run rise to the top - it doesn't matter what your race is, what your sex is, if you're disabled (there are blind poker players), etc... everyone is allowed to play and, in the long run, merit always wins.

I kind of view the world in this way, or rather that's how I think the world should be. But the world seems more complicated! In real life instead of betting actions and skill being all that matters in the long run there's so many other factors that take place. Normally it's someone's decision if you get a step up in your career, and (even this is true with poker) we don't all come from backgrounds with equal opportunities. Some people have great educations from a young age, loving parents, etc...

When you talked about the Silicon Valley companies correcting their situation by increasing education, I think that's great! Because it means they are creating a situation where people will rise to the top based on the merit they are now having the opportunity to develop. But another part of me get's stuck with the idea. I don't know if these programs are just focused on, say, people from minority races and women - but if they are, and there's a young white kid from a very disadvantaged background, with poor parenting and a lack of educational opportunities - it doesn't feel quite right that he is not given that opportunity too.

Another thing that I have a hard time with is that sometimes I feel that focusing praise around something within a channel (for example sex or race) means that people feel more incompetent than they did before. This is an advert in the UK that really bugged me recently:

It's a feminist campaign to show that girls can indeed do exercise. #thisgirlcan. My gut reaction when I saw it was that by setting the frame as 'girls can do this too' you in fact increase the gap between the sexes, because it sets the underlying tone that the gap is there. I talked about it with some male and female friends and there was a mixed response. Checking out the hashtag on Twitter (https://twitter.com/search?q=%23thisgirlcan&src=typd) the response seems to be largely super positive. So maybe I'm off the mark.

What do you think about my views?

Maurice Cherry

So here's the thing...the design and tech fields are not meritocracies. If they were, I don't think we'd really be having this conversation. But it's sure nice to think that they are, isn't it? Because thinking that absolves any sort of possible feelings of guilt based on discrimination (overt or hidden). Remember the adage "it's not what you know, but who you know"? You could be the most talented coder or designer in the game, but if you're not given a shot because of things which are outside of your control (race, gender, sexual orientation, ableness, etc.), where does that leave you? Skill helps, yes, but it's not the only deciding factor -- career advancement or otherwise.

I want to dissect something you mentioned about getting stuck on the idea of minority-focused advancement "pipeline" programs because white children may not have that same opportunity. Diversity is not a zero-sum game. These types of programs usually have pretty clear definitions on who their groups are serving. Black Girls Code is for young girls of color ages 7 to 17. Girls Who Code works with high school girls. CODE2040 works specifically with increasing the number of Black and Latino/as in the tech sector. These are important areas worth addressing as it relates to the longevity of the tech industry (and by proxy, the design industry). It would be shortsighted to look at those programs and wonder why they would work against their own missions. Not only that, just because these programs exist for these groups does not mean they are working in opposition to those who are not in those groups.

Now let's talk about the #thisgirlcan campaign you mentioned. You said that your gut reaction was that the campaign sets the tone that there is a gap between the sexes. But does it really? It's pretty evident from numerous studies and first-hand anecdotes the societal gaps that exist between men and women across a number of different areas (career advancement, pay wages, representation in government, etc.). And there are certain issues which affect women in a way that do not affect men. I took a look at the This Girl Can page, and what jumps out to me is this opening statement: "Fear of judgment is stopping many of us from taking part in exercise. But as thousands of women up and down the country are proving, it really doesn't have to." Women already have external influences (TV, magazines, etc.) pressuring them to fit a certain aesthetic ideal of beauty. Imagine if every time you went to the gym you were ogled or catcalled when you're just trying to work out. Or someone makes snide remarks because you're all sweaty and tired after leaving the gym. Those types of micro and macroaggressions can affect someone's sense of self-worth, maybe to the point of them not wanting to work out. But this campaign works to help women feel good about themselves by knowing they are not alone out there in feeling this and there's a community of support to help them. I think that's pretty damn awesome.

Josh Summers

Damn. You know when you've been looking at a thing one way all your life, and someone begins to shine a light on it from a different angle and you realise what you've been thinking has been rather muddled - that is how I feel.

I don't know if it's a mistake that other people make, but I feel like I've been trying too hard to build up mental models behind the concept of diversity. "Logically" breaking down what is in fact an incredibly complex human thing into over simplified blocks / arguments, trying to turn it into a simple maths puzzle so to speak. When in fact another, and perhaps simpler, way to look at the situation is positive action > no action. You're right, it is not zero sum.

So right now my perspective is: irrelevant to the group that you're focusing on promoting, increasing that groups confidence and their skill level is a good thing. And doing a good thing is a good thing (which does not make anyone's life outside of that group worse [and if it does, there's probably something up with them - I think the biggest cause of racism, sexism is some underling and unfounded fear in an individual]). Is that how you view the situation?

Maurice Cherry

You're right -- that's exactly how I view it. The more I talk with people for Revision Path, the more I realize that we share a lot of the same hopes, fears, dreams, and aspirations. These are hard-working people who have dedicated their time and energy to what they do. Why not give them a platform to shine?

Josh Summers

What do you feel is standing in the way of natural diversity?

Is it a demand side issue (as in prejudice is getting in the way, so some people are overlooked when they should be included, etc...)? A supply side issue (that there are not enough trained individuals, etc...)? Or some combination of the two?

Maurice Cherry

So...I think there's a few things standing in the way of natural diversity (as it relates to workplace diversity -- that's what we're talking about, right?) Businesses may not see whether or not there's a fiscal benefit to diversity. Businesses may feel that adding diversity to their business may drastically change their workplace culture. Whatever the case is, the push for diversity has to come from the top of the company.
