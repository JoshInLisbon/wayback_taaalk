Revolutionising Web Development, Introducing 'Eager'.

@zackbloom

Zack Bloom

Zack is an engineer and the cofounder of eager.io. Eager is working to bring the world of open-source libraries and tools to millions of less technical website owners. We're here to make it as easy to add comments, social sharing buttons or an image gallery to your site as it is to add an app to your smartphone. Previously Zack was a tech lead at Hubspot and has a BS in Computer Science from LTU.

Ben Romestch

Good to meet you! Obvious first question: How did the idea for Eager come about?

Zack Bloom

That's a question with a few different answers, this will be a long one. I don't think I'm used to the convo format yet, so LMK if you think I go too far afield.

My cofounder and I met as engineers at HubSpot in Boston. Together we built a good number of open source projects which were pretty popular (about 41k GitHub stars so far!). As we worked to support these projects though, we realized just how many people struggled to use the stuff we were building. We would get support issues which amounted to the user just not getting how HTML and JavaScript works. It's completely fair, they are complex tools which the average website owner shouldn't be expected to master.

The way that external scripts were included in sites hadn't changed since the birth of HTML twenty years ago. In that time though, the average website owner had transformed from a technical expert to a small business owner who was just looking for a presence on the web. These non-technical folks just weren't capable of consuming most of the open source, or even SaaS, tools out there. So they end up with less awesome websites, which use less high quality tools.

Conversely, for many SaaS businesses which need to get their embed code on a site, it's a tough world. It's actually very similar to selling computer software in the 90s. You need a sales staff, because installing the tools requires involvement from the technical team, so it's an involved process. You need to build plugins for many different website building tools, because that the only way people can install your service. There is very little discovery because there just isn't a place website owners can go to compare 'commenting' or 'analytics' tools. Often the company with the best marketing or name recognition wins.

Beyond that, we had the potential to build tech very few of these companies had. With Eager you can preview a tool on your live site without installing anything. You can place your new 'social sharing buttons' or 'image slider' exactly where you want it on your site graphically. This is just stuff that other people weren't doing, because they didn't have the time to focus on the installation process like we do.

We also feel that the writing is on the wall for simple sites which have complex backends. Your average homepage or blog should be a static site served from a CDN, not something which needs to run a hundred thousand lines of code to be served. Traditional ways of installing plugins aren't gonna work in that static world, so we wanted to see if we could build something which would.

Ben Romestch

That’s a perfect amount of content I think!

Right, next question. I’m interested in the way you are differentiating yourself from products like Google Tag Manager. Can you talk a bit about this? Is this intentional or accidental?

Zack Bloom

Nice!

Google Tag Manager and Segment are without a doubt conceptual precursors to what we're doing. The key difference being, of course, they let you include analytics tracking snippets, we let you include anything. They let marketers add tracking services without technical help, we let anyone associated with a website install a wider variety of things.

This difference led us to a more graphical experience which lets you preview apps before they're installed. We also needed a more robust way of gathering options which are required by the app being installed, and allowing users to choose locations on their site graphically.

There's also a bit of a technical difference in that they do all their includes asynchronously. In other words, you can't include anything which would normally be included in the of the page using GTM or Segment. This rules out libraries like Pace.

Finally though, there's a fundamental difference in that it's our goal to be a platform. Anyone with a source control repo can add an install.json file to their project and create an app on Eager. Adding services to the tag manager tools tends to be a more manual process on their part. Our dream is for every project to have an Install with Eager' button that allows anyone to install the project onto their site in a few clicks. This means we're gonna need thousands of apps, making us closer to a platform like the Apple App Store than a manual tool like GTM.

You asked if the similarity was accidental or intentional. In truth, I'd like to make it more intentional. From a marketing perspective it takes people a little while to figure out exactly what Eager is and what we do. It's possible that by drawing more parallels to existing tools we can make that process a bit easier.

Ben Romestch

That all makes sense to me. Have you thought about what your “A-ha” moment is, and have you done any work on trying to get new users to your site passed that point as quickly as possible? It seems like it might be hard to explain the benefits of the application to users as it is a novel idea?

Zack Bloom

I've heard it described as their being two kinds of companies, those competing in an existing market, and those creating a market. It seems like you'd want a company which doesn't have to compete, but often if there are no competitors there are no ready-made customers yet either!

Your description of getting users to an 'Ah-Ha' moment is dead on. The single biggest thing we did was allow people to preview apps on their site without installing the embed code. For example, try PACE on Taaalk: https://eager.io/app/kYKTiQjoVjQk/install?initialUrl=taaalk.co (if you already have an account try it in an incognito window). Getting people into the preview as quickly as is possible gets them invested in their site with this new addition.

Unfortunately not every app is as easy to immediately grasp as PACE. Some have to be placed into the page, or require a fair amount of customization. A big challenge for us is to make these steps simpler and more discoverable.

Ben Romestch

So do you have a 5 year goal? In a perfect world, where would you be in 5 years? In particular, where would you see revenue coming from in 5 years?

Zack Bloom

This q/a process is super interesting, it only makes me more excited about the future of Taaalk! Answering questions is so much easier than sitting down to write a blog post. I don't know how much you have left to ask, but we do have a bunch of posts on our blog which might spark more questions: https://eager.io/blog/.

One goal is to have the embed on a significant fraction of websites. I can't tell you how excited we get when we stumble upon a random site which happens to use Eager. I can't imagine how I'll feel when it's being used by 5% or 50% of the net.

Conversely, it will be an amazing feeling when most of the frontend projects we come across on GitHub have 'Install with Eager' buttons. Similarly, we can't wait for the day when we're integrated into a significant fraction of the popular website creation tools.

The dream for our users is that Eager is the place they come to when they want to add a feature to their sites. Similar models include how you open the Chrome App Store when you want to add something to your browser, or the Apple App Store when you want to add something to your phone. A user should be able to go to the Eager store, search for what they need, read reviews, make a decision, and install it on their site in a couple of minutes. That will be a world which not only empowers users, but would make it possible for someone to build a new analytics tool or SaaS business and capture a big chunk of the market without a massive marketing budget.

In terms of revenue, we help developers sell apps. We're beta testing one paid app now, check it out: https://eager.io/app/vxI8j699OODI. Like other app stores, we take a cut of the purchase price in return for handling the safe delivery of the app to client browsers everywhere. In five years, we would like to be generating a whole lot of value for our users, and in the process selling a whole lot of apps.

Even further, we would like to be a platform people use to build SaaS businesses. With us handling the discovery, billing, and getting embed codes onto pages, it would make it possible for someone out there to start a business where they can focus on building an amazing tool, not how to find users for it.

Ben Romestch

Definitely a big vision! In terms of the first steps to get there, what is your plan for getting users and selling them on the idea?

Zack Bloom

So the key insight is that people don't install an 'app store', they install an individual app. We oriented our installation experience around funneling people from existing projects to the preview (using 'Install with Eager' buttons). We then help them, from inside the preview, to install Eager and the app as one continuous flow.

Beyond that, our hope is to also have Eager integrated with as many website creation tools as is possible. Many content management tools would benefit from allowing their users to install Eager apps easily. This could come in the form of plugins to make installation easer as we do for Wordpress, or integrations into the tool itself to require no installation at all.

Ben Romestch

Do you think people have a difficult time conceptually with the idea of “apps” for their website? Is that something you have had to fight to overcome?

Zack Bloom

At first, yes. As you suggest, people don't associate the concept of apps with 'things you install onto your website'. We actually transitioned to using 'plugins' at points early in the on-boarding process. For example our homepage at the moment uses 'plugins': https://i.imgur.com/dEdkhMp.png. The initial impulse was to distance ourselves from the concept of plugins, as they are pretty strongly associated with Wordpress. Ultimately, though, effectively explaining what it is we do is more important than the theoretical risk of being mistaken for a Wordpress-specific tool.
